# The Self-Interview

## Overview
A thinking-clarification tool that asks progressively deeper questions to help you discover and articulate your own ideas. Does not produce ready-made content — instead, it extracts the content that's already in your head through structured Socratic inquiry.

## When to Use This Skill
- You have a vague idea but can't articulate it clearly
- Starting a complex piece of writing and feeling stuck
- Need to find your actual opinion on a topic
- Preparing for a presentation or talk
- Working through a strategic decision

## Process

### Round 1: Surface (5 questions)
Map the terrain of the topic:
- What's the topic in one sentence?
- Who needs to hear this and why?
- What do most people get wrong about this?
- What's your experience with this topic?
- If you could only make one point, what would it be?

### Round 2: Depth (5 questions)
Challenge assumptions and find the real insight:
- Why do you believe that? What evidence do you have?
- What's the strongest argument against your position?
- What changed your mind about this topic?
- What would surprise your audience most?
- Where does your view differ from the consensus?

### Round 3: Synthesis (3 questions)
Crystallize the core message:
- In light of everything you've said, what's the actual thesis?
- What's the one thing someone should do differently after reading this?
- How would you explain this to a smart friend over coffee?

### Output
After all three rounds, synthesize the answers into:
- A clear thesis statement
- 3-5 supporting points with evidence
- The key insight that makes this worth writing
- Suggested structure for the final piece

## Key Rules
- Never answer your own questions — the user must respond
- Wait for each answer before asking the next question
- Adapt follow-up questions based on responses
- Push back gently when answers are vague ("Can you give a specific example?")
- The goal is extraction, not generation — the ideas come from the user
