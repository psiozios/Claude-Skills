# Newsletter Ideation

## Overview
Generates 5-7 unique content angles for any topic using structured creativity frameworks. Each angle includes a hook, key points, and target audience segment. Prevents the "blank page" problem and surfaces non-obvious takes.

## When to Use This Skill
- Starting a new newsletter issue or content piece
- Feeling stuck on a topic you've covered before
- Need fresh angles on trending subjects
- Planning a content calendar

## Process

### Step 1: Topic Intake
Define the core topic and any constraints (audience, platform, length, tone).

### Step 2: Framework Rotation
Run the topic through each framework:

**SCAMPER Method:**
- **S**ubstitute: What if you replaced the common advice with its opposite?
- **C**ombine: Merge this topic with an unrelated domain
- **A**dapt: Borrow a framework from another field
- **M**odify: Exaggerate or minimize one aspect
- **P**ut to other use: Apply this to an unexpected audience
- **E**liminate: What happens if you remove the "obvious" part?
- **R**everse: Argue the contrarian position

**Jobs-to-be-Done:**
- What job is the reader hiring this content to do?
- What's the emotional progress they want to make?
- What are the social, functional, and emotional dimensions?

**Contrarian Angles:**
- What does everyone assume about this topic that might be wrong?
- What's the unpopular but defensible position?
- What's the "elephant in the room" nobody addresses?

### Step 3: Angle Output
For each angle, provide:
- **Hook**: One sentence that stops the scroll
- **Thesis**: The core argument in 2 sentences
- **Key Points**: 3-4 supporting ideas
- **Target Segment**: Who this angle resonates with most
- **Risk Level**: Safe / Moderate / Spicy

### Step 4: Selection
Rank angles by originality, relevance, and audience fit. Recommend the top pick with reasoning.

## Key Rules
- Generate at least 5 angles minimum, aim for 7
- At least one angle must be genuinely contrarian
- No generic "listicle" angles — each must have a clear thesis
- Include one angle that makes you slightly uncomfortable (that's usually the best one)
- Always consider the reader's current beliefs and challenge at least one
